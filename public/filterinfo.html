Is using a proxy
actually illegal?
And what are they
doing to your data?

Two real questions. Honest answers. No legal jargon, no scare tactics — just what you actually need to know.

The basics

No. Using a proxy is not illegal.

A proxy routes your internet traffic through another server before it reaches its destination. That's genuinely all it does. There's nothing inherently wrong with that — corporations, journalists, privacy researchers, and everyday people use them all the time.

In the United States, using a proxy is completely legal. No federal law makes it a crime. No state law makes it a crime. The technology itself is neutral. Kronos is a proxy. Kronos is legal.

Legal

Using a proxy to browse the web, access blocked content, or protect your privacy. All of it. Legal.

Not legal

Using a proxy while doing something already illegal — fraud, hacking, harassment. The proxy doesn't make the underlying crime disappear.

School filters

School filters are a policy issue,
not a legal one.

If you're using Kronos to get around your school's filters, it helps to understand what's actually at stake. Your school almost certainly has an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) — a document you agreed to when you got your school account. That document probably says you can't bypass filters.

Violating your AUP is a school discipline issue, not a criminal one. The worst realistic outcome is losing device privileges or getting a write-up — not an arrest, not a lawsuit, not a criminal record.

"The school's filter is a rule, not a law. Breaking a rule has consequences. Breaking a law does too. They are not the same thing."

Schools are legally required under the Children's Internet Protection Act (CIPA) to filter harmful content — things like explicit material. That obligation is on the school, not you. CIPA doesn't make it illegal for students to use proxies. It just means schools have to try to block them.

Where the line actually is

There are a few real limits worth knowing about.

🔒

Crimes through a proxy

If you use a proxy to do something actually illegal, the proxy doesn't protect you. The underlying act is still the problem.

📄

Terms of service

Some sites ban proxy access in their terms. Violating those can get your account banned, but it's generally not a legal issue unless money or deception is involved.

🏫

Your school's AUP

This is the main real-world thing to think about. It's a real agreement with real school-level consequences. Know what it says.

🌍

Other countries

Laws vary globally. In some countries proxy use is restricted or illegal. In the U.S., you're fine.

What schools actually use

The monitoring software on your device goes further than you probably realize.

Tools like Lightspeed and GoGuardian do a lot more than block websites. They can record your screen in real time, log browsing history, access open tabs, chat directly with individual students, and in many cases monitor activity on personal devices — not just school-issued ones.

Lightspeed, which has been replacing GoGuardian across school districts since 2024, expanded that reach dramatically. GoGuardian couldn't see your personal laptop. Lightspeed can.

"I feel that my privacy is being violated because it's my personal computer and I use the actual Chromebooks for my assessments."

— Student, Montgomery County Public Schools, quoted in The Wootton Common Sense (2024)

The Electronic Frontier Foundation filed dozens of public records requests and analyzed tens of thousands of GoGuardian flagging events. Students were flagged for visiting the text of Genesis on Bible.com, for reading Romeo and Juliet, for looking up civil rights history, and for accessing a Texas state legislature page about cannabis legislation.

"By design, GoGuardian is a red flag machine — its false positives heavily outweigh its ability to accurately determine whether the content of a site is harmful."

eff.org — How GoGuardian Invades Student Privacy (2023)

Gaggle — another competing platform — has been documented scanning student emails, chat messages, classroom assignments, journal entries, and even fictional stories. Not just during school hours. On weekends. Holidays. After midnight. Over summer break.

In some cases, this software has accessed student webcam footage while students were at home. In others, it has outed LGBTQ+ students to their families by flagging and disclosing private searches to administrators without the student's knowledge.

Your constitutional rights

The Fourth Amendment applies to you. Even at school.

Here's something schools and edtech companies rarely bring up: students have constitutional rights. The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures — and the Supreme Court has confirmed it applies inside school walls.

U.S. Constitution — Fourth Amendment

"The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated..."

In New Jersey v. T.L.O., 469 U.S. 325 (1985), the Supreme Court ruled unanimously that the Fourth Amendment applies to searches by public school officials. The Court explicitly stated that public school administrators act as agents of the state — meaning they're bound by the same constitutional limits as law enforcement.

New Jersey v. T.L.O.: The Court held that "schoolchildren have legitimate expectations of privacy" and that school searches must be (1) justified at their start by reasonable suspicion, and (2) limited in scope to the circumstances that justified them. Blanket, continuous, suspicion-less monitoring of every student's every keystroke was not what that standard contemplated.

supreme.justia.com — New Jersey v. T.L.O., 469 U.S. 325

The standard the Court set was reasonable suspicion — individualized, targeted, specific. What Lightspeed and GoGuardian do is the opposite: continuous, untargeted surveillance of millions of students, including in their own homes, with no suspicion required at all.

  • Students have a "legitimate expectation of privacy" — direct language from the Supreme Court
  • Public school officials are state actors — they're bound by the Constitution
  • Monitoring students on personal devices at home sits in a serious legal gray zone
  • FERPA gives you the right to access records your school keeps on you
  • Washington Law Review (2024) found these tools disproportionately harm LGBTQ+ students, students of color, and students with disabilities
Legal action

Courts and Congress are starting to pay attention.

In 2025, the Knight First Amendment Institute at Columbia University filed a federal lawsuit targeting how school districts use surveillance software on student laptops — seeking transparency about what data is collected, how long it's kept, and who can access it. Because right now, none of that information is publicly available.

"The transparency needed to answer [these questions] does not exist, which is why the Knight First Amendment Institute has filed a lawsuit seeking information about how one school district uses surveillance systems to monitor student laptops."

knightcolumbia.org — Knight Institute Lawsuit (2025)

Journalism students at a high school in Lawrence, Kansas successfully lobbied to have their files exempted from Gaggle's surveillance entirely — arguing that monitoring their reporting would create a chilling effect on their First Amendment right to cover school administration. It worked.

Students are pushing back on this. And it's working. Here's what you can do too.

  1. Demand transparency. Ask your district what data is collected, how long it's stored, who can see it, and whether it's ever shared with law enforcement.
  2. Know your FERPA rights. The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act gives students and parents the right to inspect educational records. Surveillance logs may qualify.
  3. Organize. Like the Kansas journalism students did. School boards respond to people who actually show up. Collective action works.
  4. Support state legislation. Several states have introduced bills limiting or banning school surveillance software. Contact your state representative.
  5. Cite the case. New Jersey v. T.L.O., 469 U.S. 325 (1985). You have a legitimate expectation of privacy. That's not an opinion — it's Supreme Court precedent.
